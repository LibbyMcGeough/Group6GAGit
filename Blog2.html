Project management in my company and my project manager

I'll give you a wee overview on my team and project management

According to Stephens Book chapter 3, a project manager has to keep team members on track and to ensure that goals are set, tracked and eventually met. However my team doesn’t have a project managers as project managers are usually in a waterfall team instead of an agile team. We as a team manage our project as we think team options and decisions are a lot better than one person’s decisions. So we don’t have assigned roles of who manages what, we usually come together and decide as a team. From chapter 3 Stephens book they seem to use a PERT chart as one form of documentation however in my team we use Confluence which is basically a text page where you can write about your project and publish it to the internal public and then a JIRA board that has all the teams tasks listed however that is only visible for our team only. We tend to keep our documentation as simple as possible so it’s done fast and its easy for others to understand and The PERT chart is hard to understand and not easy to create. As I mentioned above my team doesn’t have a project manager. We just have people in my team that manage everything together. We all have different knowledge backgrounds which helps as it all varies. Which means people can look at different arguments from different perspectives. We all have different trainings and experience. We don’t have a project manager in my team as we all agree that actually doing the job is better than telling people how to do it. So everyone in the team mostly has coding experience rather than business experience. However if anyone wanted to be a project manager it is quite simple in my team you could just ask the main manager of the team. However this will involve a lot of new training and many interviews and tests as we need to make sure the person will be good at their job.

PLANNING IN MY COMPANY

Here is a wee description on how my team plans our work

In Stephens chapter 3 it talks about using grant charts to plan projects however my team doesn’t use this technique as we don’t like guessing how long a task will take as sometimes it can go very inaccurate and cause the project being delivered late while customers are expecting it early. In fact we like the approach of Algorithmic cost modelling from Ian Somerville’s Book chapter 23.5. As it says that this approach is used to compute the project effort based on product attributes, such as size, process characteristics and experience of staff involved. We use this technique all the time. Every end of sprint we come together as a team and plan out how much points we want to give to each task in the next sprint. We all pick a number from ½ - 13. ½ meaning it’s something simple and easy to do and 13 means hard and will take a lot of effort. Then we also decide what the most critical tasks to do are and we put them as a priority on our board so they are completed first in the sprint. We like this approach a lot as it gives us the opportunity to discuss the limitations of this task as a team to compare who has what knowledge. Doing it as a team allows us to analyse the tasks better. Also like it mentions in Stephens book chapter 3, breaking a complicated tasks into small pieces that are easier to understand. My team swears by this as it makes life a lot easier for everyone in the team. Having small tasks helps you to understand what is going on and then it provides you with smaller tasks so you take less time doing them but its outcome is more effective as you don’t spend so much time on one massive task trying to understand it. In my opinion there isn't that much of a difference between the books and what my team does. Yes there is some parts that I might not agree with like i mentioned above but it all comes to the preference of your team and how they like to manage tasks. In my team we try and keep tasks as easy as possible to allow us to spend more time on the actual doing of the tasks rather than trying understand it for a long time.

Coursework planning reflection

My best technique of planning my course work is keeping one week ahead of my schedule. I find this technique good as it helps me always be on time with deadlines. This mainly works well with the CS113 class as we have stone set deadlines. However so far my plan has been working really well however sometimes I just get too busy with my work that I just cannot make the one week ahead plan which the causes stress due to deadlines coming up. So the only way you could improve this plan is by trying your best to find time to complete everything one week ahead. However with CS115 it’s hard to plan my work ahead as we can’t guarantee how long a task will take. In this case I’m technically the project manager for myself. I have a brief description of what I have to do but all the tasks and how I do it needs to be figured out by myself. So planning my project is all up to me. I have done this by a grant chart. However this is very difficult to do because I’m not sure how long each task will take. This is all because I might need training for a part of the project I don’t understand or it might take less time as I already know what to do. So from the Stephens Book chapter 3 it says “You cannot understand how tasks fit into a schedule unless you know how long those tasks will take”. I personally don’t agree with this statement as for me and also my team it’s hard to determine how long something will take. As during that process something might go wrong but also something might go ahead with no issues. So with this approach to work the best way of trying to improve it is my making sure you have the deadline in your head and make sure everything is competed by the end of June.
